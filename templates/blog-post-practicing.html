I have taken voice lessons on and off, piano lessons throughout high school, viola lessons this past semester, and have been in choirs and orchestras for almost half my life.
I have never particularly enjoyed individual practice. But still, I continue to participate in many musical activities, and I continue to practice.
This semester, while taking a class on choral conducting, I've reflected more about practicing, why I don’t enjoy it, and why it is so awesome.


Note: though I talk about practicing music in this post, it can be equally applied to any skill. One of the reasons I am not majoring in music is because individual musical practice is not something I enjoy very much. I am a computer science major because I love CS and I also love studying it and practicing it. However, music means a lot to me and I will always keep in my life - just with limited amounts of practicing.


Practicing music is frustrating for me for a few reasons.
A huge part of it is self-critique, confronting my mistakes and imperfections. I also find that the majority of my time is spent working on sections I’m not good at yet, which can be unsatisfying. These passages are often challenging and take a while to master. Practicing also requires a lot of focus - more than any of my math or computer science assignments (I can’t really zone out in the middle of a musical passage, but nothing would happen to my code).
I know that a lot my time spent programming is on tough concepts I haven't mastered, but the feeling of succesfully compiling code can keep me going for hours.
I don't get highs like these during music practice - perhaps I don't give myself enough credit for my progress.


There are also some really nice things about getting a chance to practice my ensemble pieces individually.
I get a chance to think hard about what I need to work on, at my own pace instead of the rehearsal/ensemble’s pace. If I can't sing my part, but the rest of the section can, the rehearsal won't stop for me. If I’m conducting a passage poorly but the ensemble is able to sing it well, it doesn't make sense to continue it in rehearsal, and so I cannot keep working on my gesture. Another plus - my ensemble doesn't hear me. I get to make all the mistakes I want - and no one will ever know. Practicing time is quality time with just me and the music. And this time makes a huge difference when I return to the ensemble.


When I’m in a rehearsal or performance and haven’t practiced enough, I don’t enjoy myself very much.
I often like the pieces less, finding them hard and frustrating. As a conductor, I often find myself getting stuck on when to beat and when to cue - the choristers figure it out for themselves before I can coordinate the gestures properly. Sometimes I don’t know the the piece well enough to be able to hear mistakes in the choir. I hit my multitasking limit far too quickly, before I can get to doing important things such as listening better or gesturing for phrasing.


When I have practiced, it’s wonderful.
I watch the conductor more. I’m more likely to hear the parts around me and how my part fits in. I worry less about the notes, and care more about the big picture - and therefore am much more likely to understand and appreciate the music. As a conductor, I stop worrying about the basic beating pattern or what the piece should sound like - it’s already habit - and I can focus on more interesting things like conveying tone and phrasing. When I have practiced, know a piece well, and then perform it with an ensemble, the connection between me and the other musicians is so much stronger. This connection through music is one of the best things I have ever felt. This is why I continue to excitedly join multiple ensembles each term, and why I am willing to continue to practice so I can enjoy them to the greatest extent.
